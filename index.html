Tutorial For Making An Origami Camel

  • There are two types of camels: One humped or “dromedary” camels and two humped Bactrian camels
  • Camels have three sets of eyelids and two rows of eyelashes to keep sand out of their eyes
  • Camels have thick lips which let them forage for thorny plants other animals can’t eat

Tutorial For Making An Origami Chameleon

  • Chameleons are reptiles that are part of the iguana suborder
  • Changing skin color is an important part of communication among chameleons
  • Most chameleons have a prehensile tail that they use to wrap around tree branches

Tutorial For Making An Origami Pigeon

  • Pigeons have excellent hearing abilities. They can detect sounds at far lower frequencies than humans are able to, and can thus hear distant storms and volcanoes
  • Despite the social perception as dirty and disease-ridden, pigeons are actually very clean animals and there is very little evidence to suggest that they are significant transmitters of disease
  • Pigeons can fly at altitudes up to and beyond 6000 feet, and at an average speed of 77.6 mph. The fastest recorded speed is 92.5 mph

Tutorial For Making An Origami Teddy-Bear

  • The Oxford English Dictionary dates the first use of the term teddy bear to 1906
  • In 1902 in Germany, Steiff launched “Bear 55 PB”, the first toy bear with jointed arms and legs
  • The incident of Roosevelt sparing the bear led to the popularity and naming of Teddy bears

Tutorial For Making An Origami Panda

  • Pandas are good at climbing trees and can also swim
  • An adult panda can eat 12–38 kilos of bamboo per day
  • Prehistoric pandas lived up to 2 million years ago

Tutorial For Making An Origami Flying-Cicada

  • Cicada are about the size of a grain of rice when they drop from a tree branch to the ground and start digging
  • The loud whirring or buzzing sound you hear is an all-male cicada chorus
  • Most have red-orange eyes, but occasionally cicadas have blue, white or grayish eyes
